Maarten Sap

I am an assistant professor at CMU's LTI department with a courtesy appointment in HCII, and a part-time research scientist at the Allen Institute for AI (AI2). My research focuses on endowing NLP systems with social intelligence and social commonsense, and understanding social inequality and bias in language.

Before this, I was a Postdoc/Young Investigator at the Allen Institute for AI (AI2), working on project Mosaic. I received my PhD from the University of Washington where I was advised by Noah Smith and Yejin Choi, and have interned at AI2 working on social commonsense reasoning, and at Microsoft Research working on deep learning models for understanding human cognition.

Overarching Research Themes

*Extracted by GPT-4, there may be inconsistencies.* #### *Ethics and Responsible AI Practices* My research group explores the ethical implications of artificial intelligence, focusing on fairness, accountability, and the social dynamics of AI interactions. One important paper, [Disparities in LLM Reasoning Accuracy and Explanations: A Case Study on African American English](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.04099), highlights the biases present in language models and their implications for marginalized communities. Additionally, we investigate non-verbal communication through AI in our work, [Mind the Gesture: Evaluating AI Sensitivity to Culturally Offensive Non-Verbal Gestures](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17710), emphasizing the need for cultural sensitivity in AI systems. Our research is further enriched by exploring user perceptions of AI applications, as discussed in [Diverse Perspectives on AI: Examining People's Acceptability and Reasoning of Possible AI Use Cases](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.07287). #### *Exploring Narrative Dynamics in AI* My research group explores the nuances of narrative and storytelling in artificial intelligence and its implications for understanding human experiences. One key paper, [Quantifying the Narrative Flow of Imagined Versus Autobiographical Stories](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2211715119), delves into how narratives differ based on their origins, revealing insights into personal and collective storytelling. Furthermore, we investigate empathy in narratives through our work, [HEART-felt Narratives: Tracing Empathy and Narrative Style in Personal Stories with LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17633), shedding light on emotional responses elicited by different narrative styles. Our efforts also include analyzing the variability of narrative perceptions with [The Empirical Variability of Narrative Perceptions of Social Media Texts](https://aclanthology.org/2024.emnlp-main.1113/), highlighting how digital platforms shape storytelling experiences. #### *AI Agent Social Intelligence* My research group explores the development of AI agents that exhibit social intelligence and the capacity for nuanced social interactions. In our significant work, [SOTOPIA: Interactive Evaluation for Social Intelligence in Language Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.11667), we introduce a framework for assessing an agent's social capabilities, paving the way for more empathetic interactions. We also discuss the limitations of AI's understanding of human social dynamics in [Clever Hans or Neural Theory of Mind? Stress Testing Social Reasoning in Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14763), which tests the boundaries of AI reasoning. Our ongoing research into effective interaction strategies is further explored in [SOTOPIA-S4: A User-Friendly System for Flexible, Customizable, and Large-Scale Social Simulation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.00989). #### *Enhancing Language Models with AI* My research group explores innovative techniques for improving the performance and reliability of language models. One major contribution, [Aligning LLMs to Ask Good Questions: A Case Study in Clinical Reasoning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14860), focuses on refining models to better support clinical decision-making. We are also developing tools for enhancing interaction with AI, as seen in our paper [AI-LieDar: Examine the Trade-off Between Utility and Truthfulness in LLM Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.09013), which investigates the balance between informative output and factual accuracy. Lastly, our research includes evaluating cultural adaptability, as demonstrated in our work on [NormAd: A Benchmark for Measuring the Cultural Adaptability of Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.12464), ensuring that models serve diverse user groups effectively.
